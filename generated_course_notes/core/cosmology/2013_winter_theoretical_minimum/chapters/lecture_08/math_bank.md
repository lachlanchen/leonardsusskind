## Definitions And Objects
- Baryogenesis: creation of the excess of matter over antimatter.
- Baryon number \(B\): net quark number divided by three, normalized so that a proton has \(B=1\).
- \(N_q, N_{\bar q}\): numbers of quarks and antiquarks.
- \(N_p, N_{\bar p}, N_\gamma\): numbers of protons, antiprotons, and photons.
- \(C\): charge conjugation, interchange of particle and antiparticle.
- \(P\): parity, left-right or mirror reflection.
- \(T\): time reversal.
- \(CP\): particle-antiparticle interchange plus parity.
- \(CPT\): exact symmetry theorem used in the equilibrium argument.
- Thermal equilibrium: no preferred direction of time for the microscopic bookkeeping relevant here.
- Out of equilibrium: expansion/cooling fast enough that microscopic processes do not readjust to equilibrium.
- \(a(t)\): FRW scale factor; Susskind also says \(A(t)\) on the board/transcript.
- \(H\): Hubble rate, \(H=\dot a/a\).
- \(\delta \rho/\rho\): fractional overdensity used to quantify lumpiness.
- \(\phi\): first the height coordinate in the stone analogy, then the scalar inflaton field.
- \(V(\phi)\): potential energy as a function of \(\phi\).
- \(F\): force on \(\phi\), defined by \(F=-dV/d\phi\).
- \(\gamma\): drag or viscosity coefficient in the falling-stone analogy.
- Inflaton: scalar field introduced to model inflationary dynamics.
- Cosmic friction term: the \(3H\dot{\phi}\) term in the scalar-field equation of motion.

## Derivation Steps
- Statistical-fluctuation estimate
  1. Start from an initially balanced baryon/antibaryon population.
  2. For purely random statistics, the excess scales as \(\Delta N\sim \sqrt{N}\).
  3. Take \(N\sim10^{80}\) as the rough number of baryons in the observable universe.
  4. Then \(\Delta N/N\sim10^{-40}\).
  5. Compare with the observed \(\sim10^{-8}\).
  6. Conclude that the observed baryon excess is not a mere statistical fluctuation.

- Baryon-violation-only argument fails
  1. Allow a baryon-violating process such as \(p\to e^+ + \gamma\).
  2. Include the charge-conjugate process \(\bar p\to e^- + \gamma\).
  3. If particle-antiparticle symmetry holds, the two occur with equal average weight.
  4. Include inverse thermal processes in the hot plasma.
  5. Net baryon number does not acquire a directed excess.
  6. Conclude that baryon violation alone is insufficient.

- Equilibrium obstruction from \(CPT\)
  1. Take \(CPT\) as exact.
  2. In thermal equilibrium, forward and backward time are physically equivalent for this purpose.
  3. Then the \(T\) part gives no effective bias.
  4. What remains is effectively particle-antiparticle symmetry at the level relevant to baryon counting.
  5. Therefore thermal equilibrium relaxes toward equal proton and antiproton populations.
  6. Rapid expansion/cooling is needed to evade this.

- Falling stone with viscous drag
  1. Introduce a coordinate \(\phi\) for the stone’s height.
  2. Define the potential \(V(\phi)\).
  3. Take the force to be \(F=-dV/d\phi\).
  4. Set the mass to unity.
  5. Without drag, write \(\ddot\phi=F\).
  6. Add a drag force proportional to velocity and opposite to the motion.
  7. Write the cleaned equation as \(\ddot\phi+\gamma \dot\phi=F\).
  8. Set \(\ddot\phi=0\) to get terminal motion.
  9. Obtain \(\dot\phi_{\mathrm{term}}=F/\gamma\), with sign determined by \(F\).

- Scalar-field equation in an expanding box
  1. Assume the scalar field is homogeneous, so spatial gradients are neglected.
  2. Take the homogeneous energy density as \(\rho_\phi=\frac12\dot\phi^2+V(\phi)\).
  3. Use a comoving unit box with physical volume \(a^3\).
  4. Multiply by \(a^3\) to get the box energy.
  5. Form the Lagrangian \(L=a^3(\frac12\dot\phi^2-V)\).
  6. Compute \(\partial L/\partial \dot\phi=a^3\dot\phi\).
  7. Compute \(\partial L/\partial \phi=-a^3\,dV/d\phi\).
  8. Apply Euler-Lagrange: \(\frac{d}{dt}(a^3\dot\phi)=-a^3\,dV/d\phi\).
  9. Expand the time derivative: \(a^3\ddot\phi+3a^2\dot a\,\dot\phi=-a^3\,dV/d\phi\).
  10. Divide by \(a^3\).
  11. Identify \(\dot a/a=H\).
  12. Get \(\ddot\phi+3H\dot\phi+\frac{dV}{d\phi}=0\), or equivalently \(\ddot\phi+3H\dot\phi=F\).

## Notation Choices
- Use \(N_q\) and \(N_{\bar q}\), not \(N_g\) or other ambiguous handwritten alternatives.
- Use \(B\) for baryon number and normalize it by \(B=(N_q-N_{\bar q})/3\).
- Use \(N_p\), \(N_{\bar p}\), and \(N_\gamma\) for proton, antiproton, and photon counts.
- Use \(e^+\), \(e^-\), \(\mu^+\), \(\mu^-\), and \(\gamma\) for the decay products.
- Use \(C\), \(P\), \(T\), \(CP\), and \(CPT\) as symmetry labels/operators, not as generic prose abbreviations.
- Use \(\phi\) consistently for both the analogy coordinate and the scalar field; do not switch to \(\varphi\) unless required by surrounding notation.
- Use \(a(t)\) in the cleaned notes for the scale factor, with a brief note if needed that Susskind says or writes \(A(t)\) in this portion.
- Use \(H=\dot a/a\) and call \(3H\dot\phi\) the cosmic friction term.
- Use a dot for time derivatives: \(\dot\phi\), \(\ddot\phi\), \(\dot a\).
- Use the ordinary derivative \(dV/d\phi\) where following the lecture closely; allow \(V'(\phi)\) only if explicitly identified as the same object.
- Represent time reversal schematically as \(t\to -t\) together with complex conjugation of the wavefunction.
- Keep the B-meson example qualitative in notation; do not lock the chapter to exact decay-channel labels that Susskind himself treats uncertainly.

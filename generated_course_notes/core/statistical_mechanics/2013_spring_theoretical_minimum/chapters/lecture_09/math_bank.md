## Definitions And Objects
- \(z\): one-spin partition function for the simple magnet.
- \(Z\): full partition function for the interacting chain.
- \(\sigma_i\): Ising spin at site \(i\), taking values \(\pm1\).
- \(j\): coupling/field-strength parameter used by Susskind on the board; keep lowercase unless the chapter has already standardized otherwise.
- \(\beta\): inverse temperature.
- \(\mu_i\): bond variable between neighboring spins, \(\mu_i=\sigma_i\sigma_{i+1}\).
- Correlation function: the lecture uses \(\langle \sigma_i\sigma_{i+n}\rangle\) as the diagnostic of long-range memory.
- \(d\): spatial dimension; a hypercubic lattice has \(2d\) nearest neighbors.
- \(\bar{\sigma}\): average spin over the lattice; Susskind explicitly switches to bar notation instead of angle brackets.
- \(\bar{\bar{\sigma}}\): temporary notation for the average of one distinguished spin in the mean field generated by the others.
- Mean-field approximation: replace the neighbor sum seen by one spin by its average value \(2d\,\bar{\sigma}\).
- \(y\): reduced variable \(y=2\beta d j\,\bar{\sigma}\), introduced to simplify the graphical solution.

## Derivation Steps
### One-spin partition function and averages
1. Start from the single-spin energy \(E=-j\,\sigma\) with \(\sigma=\pm1\).
2. Write the Boltzmann sum over the two configurations.
3. Evaluate \(z=e^{\beta j}+e^{-\beta j}=2\cosh(\beta j)\).
4. Use \(\langle E\rangle=-\partial_\beta \log z=-\frac{1}{z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial \beta}\).
5. Differentiate \(2\cosh(\beta j)\) with respect to \(\beta\).
6. Divide by \(z\) to get \(\langle E\rangle=-j\,\sinh(\beta j)/\cosh(\beta j)\).
7. Simplify to \(\langle E\rangle=-j\,\tanh(\beta j)\).
8. Since \(E=-j\,\sigma\), infer \(\langle \sigma\rangle=\tanh(\beta j)\).

### Bond-variable solution of the 1D Ising model
1. Start from \(E=-j\sum_i \sigma_i\sigma_{i+1}\).
2. Fix the first spin temporarily and define \(\mu_i=\sigma_i\sigma_{i+1}\).
3. Note that knowing the first spin and all \(\mu_i\) reconstructs the whole chain.
4. Rewrite the energy as \(E=-j\sum_i \mu_i\).
5. Observe that the Boltzmann factor factorizes into independent bond contributions.
6. Sum independently over each \(\mu_i=\pm1\).
7. Restore the missing factor \(2\) from the two choices of the first spin.
8. Obtain \(Z=2[2\cosh(\beta j)]^{N-1}\).

### Nearest-neighbor correlation
1. Treat \(\mu_i\) as the effective one-bond degree of freedom.
2. Reuse the one-spin calculation with \(\mu_i=\pm1\).
3. Compute \(\langle \mu_i\rangle=\tanh(\beta j)\).
4. Translate back to spins: \(\langle \sigma_i\sigma_{i+1}\rangle=\tanh(\beta j)\).

### Long-distance correlation
1. Begin with \(\langle \sigma_i\sigma_{i+n}\rangle\).
2. Insert factors of \(1=\sigma_{i+k}^2\) for intermediate sites.
3. Regroup the product into bond variables: \(\sigma_i\sigma_{i+n}=\mu_i\mu_{i+1}\cdots\mu_{i+n-1}\).
4. Use independence of the bond variables in the factorized measure.
5. Replace each \(\langle \mu\rangle\) by \(\tanh(\beta j)\).
6. Get \(\langle \sigma_i\sigma_{i+n}\rangle=[\tanh(\beta j)]^n\), once the distance convention is fixed.
7. Read off exponential decay for any finite temperature because \(|\tanh(\beta j)|<1\) for finite \(\beta\).

### Mean-field reduction in \(d\) dimensions
1. Focus on one spin and write its local energy as \(E_{\text{local}}=-j\,\sigma\sum_{\text{neighbors}}\sigma_{\text{neighbor}}\).
2. Assume the lattice has average spin \(\bar{\sigma}\).
3. Replace the sum over the \(2d\) neighbors by \(2d\,\bar{\sigma}\).
4. Get the effective one-spin energy \(E_{\text{mf}}=-2dj\,\bar{\sigma}\,\sigma\).
5. Reuse the one-spin result with effective coupling \(2dj\,\bar{\sigma}\).
6. Conclude that the average of the distinguished spin is \(\bar{\bar{\sigma}}=\tanh(2\beta d j\,\bar{\sigma})\).
7. Impose self-consistency by translation invariance: \(\bar{\bar{\sigma}}=\bar{\sigma}\).
8. Obtain the implicit equation \(\bar{\sigma}=\tanh(2\beta d j\,\bar{\sigma})\).

### Graphical phase-transition criterion
1. Define \(y=2\beta d j\,\bar{\sigma}\).
2. Rewrite the self-consistency equation as \(y/(2\beta d j)=\tanh y\).
3. Interpret the left-hand side as a straight line through the origin with slope \(1/(2\beta d j)\).
4. Interpret the right-hand side as the \(\tanh y\) curve.
5. At high temperature the line is steep, so the only intersection is at \(y=0\).
6. Lower the temperature until the line is tangent to \(\tanh y\) at the origin.
7. Use \(\tanh'(0)=1\) to get \(1/(2\beta_c d j)=1\).
8. Below that temperature, nonzero intersections appear, giving nonzero average magnetization.

### External-field branch selection
1. Add an external-field term to the mean-field energy.
2. Choose the sign convention so positive \(b\) favors up spins.
3. Recompute the one-spin average in the shifted effective field.
4. Obtain \(\bar{\sigma}=\tanh(2\beta d j\,\bar{\sigma}+\beta b)\).
5. Keep the same definition \(y=2\beta d j\,\bar{\sigma}\).
6. Rewrite as \(y/(2\beta d j)=\tanh(y+\beta b)\).
7. Interpret the new equation as a horizontal shift of the \(\tanh\) curve, or equivalently a displaced family of lines.
8. See that for \(b\neq0\) the symmetric origin solution is removed as the relevant branch.
9. Let \(b\to0^\pm\) after the thermodynamic reasoning to identify the magnetized branches and the instability of the zero-magnetization state.

### Low-temperature domain argument for why \(d=1\) fails
1. Start from the all-aligned ground state at very low temperature.
2. In \(1d\), flip one spin and count two broken bonds.
3. Flip any adjacent block of spins and note that the energy cost is still only two broken bonds.
4. Count the many possible placements of such blocks.
5. Conclude that long flipped domains are entropically cheap in one dimension.
6. In \(2d\), flip one spin and count four broken bonds.
7. Grow the flipped region and note that the energy cost grows with the boundary length.
8. Conclude that higher-dimensional domains are much more expensive, which is the missing physics not captured by naive \(d=1\) use of the mean-field formula.

## Notation Choices
- Use lowercase \(j\), not \(J\), unless the surrounding chapter has already standardized otherwise.
- Use lowercase \(d\) for dimension, even though the transcript sometimes says “D” verbally.
- Use \(z\) for the one-spin partition function and \(Z\) for the full many-spin partition function.
- Use \(\bar{\sigma}\) for the average spin in the mean-field section, because Susskind explicitly endorses bar notation over angle brackets there.
- It is still acceptable to identify \(\bar{\sigma}=\langle \sigma\rangle\) once, then use the bar consistently.
- Keep \(\bar{\bar{\sigma}}\) only as a temporary intermediate notation for the distinguished spin if the chapter wants to mirror the lecture’s logic; otherwise introduce it briefly and eliminate it quickly.
- Write \(\tanh\), not “tanch.”
- Treat “magnetization” and “average spin” as the same scalar quantity in this lecture.
- For long-distance correlation, define the distance convention explicitly:
  - If \(n\) means bond separation, use \(\langle \sigma_i\sigma_{i+n}\rangle=[\tanh(\beta j)]^n\).
  - If \(n\) means site-index difference as spoken in the hesitant classroom exchange, adjust the exponent accordingly, but choose one convention and keep it fixed.
- In the mean-field graph, use \(T=1/\beta\) only after first deriving the condition in \(\beta\)-language; that matches the lecture’s rhythm.
- For the external field, present one coherent sign convention:
  - choose \(E_b=-b\,\sigma\) for \(b>0\) favoring up spins,
  - then the self-consistency equation is \(\bar{\sigma}=\tanh(2\beta d j\,\bar{\sigma}+\beta b)\).

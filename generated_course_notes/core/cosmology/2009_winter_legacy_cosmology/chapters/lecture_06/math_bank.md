## Definitions And Objects
- Surface of last scattering: the transition surface where the universe changes from opaque ionized plasma to transparent neutral matter.
- Ionization threshold of hydrogen: described qualitatively as a few thousand kelvin; this is the temperature scale for the opacity transition.
- Adiabatic expansion: expansion slow compared with microscopic reaction rates; entropy is approximately conserved.
- Thermal equilibrium: forward and inverse reactions both operative enough to maintain equilibrium abundances.
- One-way street: the regime after inverse production becomes thermally suppressed while annihilation still proceeds.
- Charge neutrality: equality of total positive and negative charge, not equality of baryons and antibaryons.
- Charge conjugation \(C\): replacement of each particle by its antiparticle.
- Parity \(P\): spatial reflection; enters only when the lecture sharpens condition 2 to CP violation.
- Time reversal \(T\): reversal of the direction of time in the dynamical history.
- CPT theorem: simultaneous particle-antiparticle replacement and time reversal, with parity included in the full theorem, is treated as exact within relativistic quantum field theory.
- Baryon number \(B\): net number of baryons minus antibaryons; in lecture practice often simplified to proton number minus antiproton number.
- Baryogenesis: the generation of a small nonzero baryon asymmetry from an initially symmetric state.
- Residual asymmetry: the leftover \(N_p-N_{\bar p}\) after proton-antiproton annihilation freezes out.

## Derivation Steps
Pair-production threshold and abundance:
1. Two photons can create an \(e^-e^+\) pair only if their total available energy reaches the rest-mass threshold.
2. Just above threshold, photons still dominate because massive pairs are expensive to produce.
3. Far above threshold, the rest mass becomes less important than thermal kinetic energy.
4. At that stage, photons, electrons, and positrons have comparable abundances.

Proton threshold and transition to quark language:
1. The proton rest energy is about \(2000\) times the electron rest energy.
2. Therefore the temperature needed to create \(p\bar p\) pairs is much higher than the electron-positron threshold.
3. By the time that scale is reached, the proton is no longer the best degree of freedom.
4. The lecture therefore switches to quarks and antiquarks as the more faithful high-temperature language.
5. For exposition, it then drops back to proton/antiproton language when discussing baryon asymmetry.

Freeze-out as a one-way street:
1. In the hot phase, \(p\bar p\) annihilation and inverse production can both occur.
2. As expansion lowers photon energies, inverse production eventually falls below threshold.
3. Direct annihilation still proceeds because the existing \(p\) and \(\bar p\) can find each other in the dense soup.
4. The net flow then runs from \(p,\bar p\) into photons.
5. The same logic later applies to \(e^-e^+\) pairs.

Photon counting and the one-part-in-a-billion asymmetry:
1. In adiabatic expansion, entropy is approximately conserved.
2. In this lecture’s approximation, entropy is proportional to the total number of particles up to order-one factors.
3. Annihilated matter-antimatter pairs are therefore replaced mainly by photons rather than by a huge loss of particle count.
4. Today’s photon abundance records the earlier annihilation history.
5. Since \(N_\gamma/N_p \sim 10^9\text{--}10^{10}\), the primordial excess was only about one extra proton per \(10^9\) proton-antiproton pairs.

Why ordinary cooling cannot generate the asymmetry within tested physics:
1. Start from \(N_p = N_{\bar p}\).
2. To end with one extra proton, one must have a process that changes net baryon number.
3. Electric neutrality also requires a compensating negatively charged particle such as an electron.
4. The tested standard-model rules do not allow proton creation without simultaneous antiproton creation.
5. Therefore ordinary low-energy cooling cannot generate the observed net baryon excess.

Why baryon-number violation alone is not enough:
1. Suppose baryon number is violated.
2. That only means processes can change the net baryon count.
3. Without a bias, proton-producing and antiproton-producing channels occur with equal average weight.
4. The mean asymmetry still vanishes.
5. A second ingredient is therefore needed: particle-antiparticle asymmetry in the laws.

Why nonequilibrium is needed:
1. CPT implies a deep relation between particle-antiparticle exchange and time reversal.
2. In thermal equilibrium, forward and backward histories are effectively indistinguishable.
3. Under those conditions, the CPT relation forces particle and antiparticle abundances back into symmetry.
4. Therefore a persistent baryon asymmetry requires a stage when the universe is not in equilibrium.
5. In the lecture this is identified with sufficiently rapid early expansion.

Statistical fluctuation estimate:
1. If there were no bias, random excesses would scale like \(\sqrt{N}\).
2. For the observable universe, \(N\) is of order \(10^{90}\) including photons.
3. That gives fluctuations of order \(10^{45}\).
4. The actual proton count is of order \(10^{80}\).
5. Statistical patchiness is therefore far too small to explain the observed matter excess.

Neutron-decay side calculation:
1. Free-neutron beta decay is allowed because the neutron is slightly heavier than the final state.
2. The available energy is small because \(m_n\) is only slightly above \(m_p+m_e\) in practice.
3. Small phase space helps explain the long neutron lifetime.
4. In a bound nucleus, binding energy lowers the initial-state mass.
5. That can remove the available decay energy and stabilize the neutron.

## Notation Choices
- Use \(e^-\) and \(e^+\) for electron and positron.
- Use \(p,\bar p\) and \(n,\bar n\) for proton, antiproton, neutron, antineutron.
- Use \(q,\bar q\) for quark and antiquark when discussing the hotter soup.
- Use \(\gamma\) for photon in equations, even if the lecture draws wavy lines on the board.
- Use \(N_X\) for total number of species \(X\); use \(n_X\) for number density when writing equilibrium comparisons.
- Keep the lecture’s board notation \(N_p\), \(N_{\bar p}\), and \(N_p-N_{\bar p}\) rather than introducing a new asymmetry symbol.
- If a compact asymmetry variable is later needed, introduce it only after first writing the lecture’s explicit counts.
- Use a bar to denote antiparticles throughout; do not mix bar notation with “anti-” inside equations.
- State baryon number first in the explicit proton/neutron form, then optionally abbreviate as \(B=N_b-N_{\bar b}\).
- Condition 2 should be named finally as CP violation, while noting that the spoken development passes first through charge-conjugation language.
- Condition 3 should be called out of equilibrium or nonequilibrium, not CPT violation.
- Keep “adiabatic” in the lecture’s sense: slow compared with microscopic reaction rates.

## Uncertain Mathematics
- The threshold formulas \(E_{\gamma\gamma}\ge 2mc^2\) are lecture-level shorthand, not a fully invariant center-of-mass derivation.
- The lower board note in the threshold sketch is best read as \(2m_p c^2\), but that reading is less secure than \(2m_e c^2\).
- The board equality \(N_p=N_{\bar p}\) is clear in the later frame and only partial in the earlier one; use it confidently but cite the board cautiously.
- The lecture oscillates between \(10^9\) and \(10^{10}\) photons per proton; the final notes should preserve this as order-of-magnitude language.
- “Entropy is the number of particles” should be stated cautiously as an order-one thermal-gas approximation, not an exact identity.
- The CPT/equilibrium argument is conceptual in the lecture; do not over-formalize it into an unmotivated theorem proof.
- The proton lifetime figures are lecture-era numbers and should be presented as the lecture’s quoted scale, not as a newly normalized modern limit.
- The neutron-decay tail is transcript-noisy and not central to the chapter spine; if retained, normalize the mass statement to \(m_e\) rather than leaning on the transcript’s sign-language inconsistency.
- The electroweak-symmetry-breaking temperature around \(100\text{--}250\,\mathrm{GeV}\) appears only in Q&A and should remain secondary if included at all.
- The lecture’s “pair of photons make a proton and an electron” examples are schematic illustrations of baryon-number-violating channels, not standard-model processes to be presented as established reactions.

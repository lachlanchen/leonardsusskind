## Definitions And Objects
- Best coordinates at a point: the locally most Cartesian coordinates, where the metric equals the Kronecker delta at that point and first derivatives of the metric vanish there.
- Covariant derivative: the tensorial derivative operator constructed so that, in best coordinates at a point, it reduces to the ordinary derivative.
- Christoffel symbols \(\Gamma^{p}{}_{rs}\): built from first derivatives of the metric; vanish at a point in best coordinates; not tensors.
- Covariant change \(dV^n\): the infinitesimal vector change measured in the local best coordinates.
- Parallel transport: motion of a vector along a curve with vanishing covariant change.
- Geodesic: a curve whose tangent vector is parallel to itself; also described as a stationary-distance curve, but the lecture prefers the local “straightest possible” definition.
- Tangent vector \(t^m\): the unit vector \(dx^m/ds\) along a curve.
- Proper time \(\tau\): the spacetime interval parameter used in the Minkowski and relativistic geodesic discussion.
- Minkowski coordinates: special-relativistic analog of Cartesian coordinates.
- \(\eta_{\mu\nu}\): flat Minkowski metric in special coordinates.
- \(g_{\mu\nu}(x)\): general spacetime metric, now allowed to depend on spacetime position.
- Hyperbolic angle \(\omega\): time-like coordinate in the accelerated-coordinate construction.
- Proper acceleration: acceleration measured with respect to proper time, not coordinate time.
- \(R\): a chosen reference hyperbola with proper acceleration \(g\).
- \(y\): local spatial displacement away from the reference hyperbola \(r=R\).
- \(\Phi\): Newtonian gravitational potential per unit mass; here \(\Phi=gy\) in the uniform-field approximation.

## Derivation Steps
Covariant derivative of a contravariant vector
1. Start with \(V^n=g^{np}V_p\).
2. Apply the covariant derivative and use the product rule: \(\nabla_m(g^{np}V_p)=(\nabla_m g^{np})V_p+g^{np}\nabla_m V_p\).
3. Use \(\nabla_m g^{np}=0\).
4. Insert the known formula for \(\nabla_m V_p\).
5. Rearrange indices to obtain \(\nabla_m V^n=\partial_m V^n+\Gamma^{n}{}_{mr}V^r\).

Covariant change and parallel transport
1. Begin with \(\nabla_m V^n=\partial_m V^n+\Gamma^{n}{}_{mr}V^r\).
2. Multiply by \(dx^m\) to get the change along an infinitesimal displacement.
3. Identify \(dV^n=\partial_m V^n\,dx^m\).
4. Obtain \(dV^n+\Gamma^{n}{}_{mr}V^r\,dx^m\).
5. Set this to zero to define parallel transport.

Geodesic equation from tangent parallel transport
1. Define the tangent vector \(t^m=dx^m/ds\).
2. Impose parallel transport on the tangent itself: \(dt^n+\Gamma^{n}{}_{mr}t^r\,dx^m=0\).
3. Divide by \(ds\).
4. Replace \(dx^m/ds\) by \(t^m\).
5. Get \(\dfrac{dt^n}{ds}=-\Gamma^{n}{}_{mr}t^r t^m\).
6. Use \(t^n=dx^n/ds\) to rewrite this as \(\dfrac{d^2x^n}{ds^2}=-\Gamma^{n}{}_{mr}\dfrac{dx^r}{ds}\dfrac{dx^m}{ds}\).

Minkowski “polar” coordinates
1. Introduce \(x=r\cosh\omega\), \(t=r\sinh\omega\).
2. Use \(\cosh^2\omega-\sinh^2\omega=1\).
3. Conclude \(x^2-t^2=r^2\), so fixed \(r\) gives a hyperbola.
4. Interpret \(\omega\) as the coordinate that advances along the hyperbola.
5. Interpret \(r\) as the spatial label of different hyperbolas.

Metric in accelerated coordinates
1. Start from \(d\tau^2=dt^2-dx^2\) in the reduced \(1+1\) dimensional discussion.
2. Substitute \(x=r\cosh\omega\), \(t=r\sinh\omega\).
3. Differentiate \(x\) and \(t\).
4. Expand \(dt^2-dx^2\).
5. Use \(\cosh^2\omega-\sinh^2\omega=1\) and cancellation of cross terms.
6. Obtain \(d\tau^2=r^2\,d\omega^2-dr^2\).

Local near-Earth expansion
1. Choose a reference hyperbola \(r=R\) with \(g=c^2/R\), or \(g=1/R\) if \(c=1\).
2. Write \(r=R+y\), so \(dr=dy\).
3. Substitute into \(d\tau^2=r^2 d\omega^2-dr^2\).
4. Expand \((R+y)^2=R^2+2Ry+y^2\).
5. Factor \(R^2\): \(R^2+2Ry+y^2=R^2(1+2y/R+y^2/R^2)\).
6. Define \(t=R\omega\), so \(R^2 d\omega^2=dt^2\).
7. Get \(d\tau^2=\left(1+\frac{2y}{R}+\frac{y^2}{R^2}\right)dt^2-dy^2\).
8. Drop \(y^2/R^2\) in the local weak-field region.
9. Use \(g=1/R\) to obtain \(d\tau^2\approx (1+2gy)dt^2-dy^2\).

Newtonian limit from the geodesic equation
1. Start with \(\dfrac{d^2y}{d\tau^2}=-\Gamma^{y}{}_{mr}\dfrac{dx^r}{d\tau}\dfrac{dx^m}{d\tau}\).
2. Assume slow motion, so spatial components of four-velocity are small.
3. Keep only the \(tt\) term: \(\dfrac{d^2y}{d\tau^2}\approx -\Gamma^{y}{}_{tt}\).
4. Compute \(\Gamma^{y}{}_{tt}\) from the metric.
5. Since \(g_{yy}=+1\) and mixed \(yt\) terms vanish, only the \(\partial_y g_{tt}\) term survives.
6. Use the corrected standard formula to get \(\Gamma^{y}{}_{tt}=\frac12\,\partial_y g_{tt}\).
7. Insert \(g_{tt}=-(1+2gy)\).
8. Then \(\partial_y g_{tt}=-2g\), so \(\Gamma^{y}{}_{tt}=-g\).
9. Therefore \(\dfrac{d^2y}{d\tau^2}=-\Gamma^{y}{}_{tt}=-(-g)\) must be checked against the lecture’s sign convention at the metric level.
10. Normalizing to the lecture’s final physical result gives \(\dfrac{d^2y}{d\tau^2}=-g\).

Weak-field potential identification
1. Read the local metric as \(d\tau^2\approx (1+2gy)dt^2-dy^2\).
2. Recognize \(gy\) as the Newtonian potential per unit mass in a uniform field.
3. Therefore the coefficient of \(dt^2\) is \(1+2\Phi\).
4. This motivates the more general weak-field rule that \(g_{tt}\) encodes the Newtonian potential.

Schwarzschild preview heuristic
1. Replace the uniform-field potential by a point-mass potential \(\Phi(y)\sim -GM/y\).
2. Guess that the time-time metric coefficient should take the weak-field form \(1+2\Phi\).
3. This suggests a coefficient like \(1-2GM/y\).
4. Observe that it vanishes at \(y=2GM\).
5. Use that vanishing only as a preview of horizon structure, not as a derivation.

## Notation Choices
- Use “best coordinates” as the main term in the notes. Mention once that Susskind begins with “Gaussian normal coordinates” but then corrects himself.
- Use Latin indices \(m,n,r,s,p\) for the curved-space review before the explicit spacetime pivot.
- Use Greek indices \(\mu,\nu\) only once the lecture moves to spacetime.
- Use \(g_{mn}\), \(g_{\mu\nu}\), and \(g^{mn}\), \(g^{\mu\nu}\) as the normalized notation, even though the transcript verbally alternates between uppercase and lowercase “G”.
- Use \(\Gamma^{p}{}_{rs}\) for Christoffel symbols with one upper, two lower indices.
- Use \(V_s\) for a covector and \(V^n\) for a vector.
- Use \(dV^n\) for the covariant infinitesimal change along a displacement, matching the lecture’s operational presentation.
- Use \(t^m\) for the tangent vector in the geometry section, since that is what the board and transcript support.
- Use \(s\) for spatial line-element parameter in the purely geometric part, and \(\tau\) for proper time in spacetime.
- Preserve the lecture’s convention \(d\tau^2=-g_{\mu\nu}dx^\mu dx^\nu\) together with \(g_{\mu\nu}=\mathrm{diag}(-1,1,1,1)\).
- Reserve \(\eta_{\mu\nu}\) for the flat Minkowski metric in special coordinates.
- In the accelerated-coordinate section, use \(r,\omega\) first, then introduce \(R\) as a fixed reference hyperbola, \(y\) as local displacement, and \(t=R\omega\) as the local time coordinate.
- Use \(g\) for the near-Earth gravitational acceleration, with \(g=1/R\) in \(c=1\) units and \(g=c^2/R\) when units are restored.
- Use \(\Phi\) for the Newtonian gravitational potential per unit mass when summarizing the weak-field identification.

## Uncertain Mathematics
- The lecture’s live derivation of the Christoffel formula contains an index correction midstream. The final notes should present only the corrected standard formula.
- The transcript’s Euler-form expression for \(\sin\theta\) is missing the \(i\) in the denominator at first pass; this must be normalized to the standard formula.
- The hyperbolic-identity segment is garbled in the transcript. The mathematically correct identity is \(\cosh^2\omega-\sinh^2\omega=1\).
- The transcript briefly stumbles when interpreting \(r\) and \(\omega\); the final notes should clearly fix \(\omega\) as time-like and \(r\) as space-like.
- In the local metric expansion, the transcript occasionally says “minus the \(r^2\)” where the intended term is \(-dy^2\) after \(dr=dy\). This should be normalized carefully.
- The board in `lecture_04_figure_05.png` does not fully expose the trailing factors of the expanded metric. \(d\omega^2\) and \(-dy^2\) are justified by transcript plus standard reconstruction, not by direct full legibility.
- The board in `lecture_04_figure_06.png` visibly writes a line like \(\dfrac{d^2y}{d\tau^2}=+\partial_y g_{tt}\), but the transcript around it includes sign confusion and a factor-of-\(\tfrac12\) discussion. The final notes should not reproduce that chalk line literally.
- The precise weak-field sign relation between \(\Gamma^{y}{}_{tt}\), \(\partial_y g_{tt}\), and the final \(-g\) must be stated in a convention-aware way. The safest final formulation is the physically normalized result \(\dfrac{d^2y}{d\tau^2}=-g\), with the intermediate sign bookkeeping explained cautiously.
- The Schwarzschild discussion at the end is heuristic. The chapter should present it as a motivated guess from \(1+2\Phi\), not as a derivation of the Schwarzschild metric.

## Definitions And Objects

- Bosonic two-particle state: wavefunction symmetric under interchange of particle labels.
- Fermionic two-particle state: wavefunction antisymmetric under interchange of particle labels.
- In the two-hydrogen discussion, \(x_1,x_2\) label electron coordinates and \(y_1,y_2\) label proton coordinates.
- A hydrogen-atom one-body wavefunction is a function of one electron coordinate and one proton coordinate, e.g. \(\psi(x_1,y_1)\).
- Half-spin state space: two-dimensional spin space, distinct from ordinary space.
- Spinor for spin \(1/2\): two-component column \((\alpha,\beta)^T\), with upper entry tied to \(z\)-up and lower entry to \(z\)-down.
- Pauli matrices \(\sigma_i\): the \(2\times2\) matrices without the factor \(\tfrac12\).
- Spin matrices \(S_i\): the operators \(\tfrac12\sigma_i\) for spin \(1/2\).
- Spin-1 state space: three-dimensional, with \(m_s=+1,0,-1\).
- Bra vector: Hermitian conjugate of a ket; used explicitly in the spin-1 probability discussion.
- Dirac spinor \(\Psi\): four-component wavefunction, organized as two two-component blocks.
- Dirac-matrix block structure: one \(2\times2\) doubling for energy sign, one \(2\times2\) doubling for spin.
- Positive/negative energy combinations in the rest frame: \(\psi_++\psi_-\) and \(\psi_+-\psi_-\).
- Dirac sea picture: vacuum obtained by filling all negative-energy electron states.
- Positron: hole in the filled negative-energy sea.

## Derivation Steps

Two-particle symmetrization / antisymmetrization

1. Start with an arbitrary two-particle wavefunction \(\phi(x,y)\).
2. Exchange the labels \(x\leftrightarrow y\) to get \(\phi(y,x)\).
3. Form \(\phi(x,y)+\phi(y,x)\); this is unchanged by interchange.
4. Form \(\phi(x,y)-\phi(y,x)\); this changes sign under interchange.
5. Use the symmetric form for bosons and the antisymmetric form for fermions.

Two hydrogen atoms as four fermions

1. Start from a product state \(\psi(x_1,y_1)\phi(x_2,y_2)\).
2. Antisymmetrize in the electron labels \(x_1\leftrightarrow x_2\).
3. Take that result and antisymmetrize in the proton labels \(y_1\leftrightarrow y_2\).
4. Obtain the four-term wavefunction with signs \(+,-,-,+\).
5. Exchange the two whole atoms \((x_1,y_1)\leftrightarrow(x_2,y_2)\).
6. Note that this exchange is equivalent to exchanging the \(x\)'s and the \(y\)'s together.
7. Get one minus sign from the electron exchange and one minus sign from the proton exchange.
8. Conclude that the whole two-atom wavefunction is symmetric under exchange of the two atoms.
9. Set \(x_1=x_2\) or \(y_1=y_2\) and see the antisymmetrized expression vanish.

Spin-\(\tfrac12\): \(z\), \(x\), and \(y\) eigenvectors

1. Choose \(S_z\) diagonal, equivalently \(\sigma_z\) diagonal.
2. Solve \(\sigma_z(\alpha,\beta)^T=+(\alpha,\beta)^T\); get \(\beta=0\).
3. Normalize to \(|+\!z\rangle=(1,0)^T\).
4. Solve \(\sigma_z(\alpha,\beta)^T=-(\alpha,\beta)^T\); get \(\alpha=0\).
5. Normalize to \(|-\!z\rangle=(0,1)^T\).
6. Solve \(\sigma_x(\alpha,\beta)^T=+(\alpha,\beta)^T\); matrix action swaps the entries, so \(\alpha=\beta\).
7. Normalize to \(|+\!x\rangle=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(1,1)^T\).
8. Solve \(\sigma_x(\alpha,\beta)^T=-(\alpha,\beta)^T\); get \(\beta=-\alpha\).
9. Normalize to \(|-\!x\rangle=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(1,-1)^T\).
10. Solve the \(\sigma_y\) eigenvalue equations; get spinors proportional to \((1,\pm i)^T\).
11. Read the nontrivial phase \(i\) as the new ingredient distinguishing \(y\)-polarization from \(x\)-polarization.

Spin-1 eigenvectors in the chosen representation

1. Use the chosen \(3\times3\) matrices \(S_x^{(1)},S_y^{(1)},S_z^{(1)}\).
2. Do not diagonalize the matrices by choice beforehand; solve the eigenvalue problem directly.
3. Solve \(S_z^{(1)}v=+v\); get \(v\propto(1,-i,0)^T\).
4. Solve \(S_z^{(1)}v=-v\); get \(v\propto(1,i,0)^T\).
5. Solve \(S_z^{(1)}v=0\); get \(v\propto(0,0,1)^T\).
6. Normalize the first two with \(1/\sqrt2\).

Probability extraction for spin-1 components

1. Write the state as \(\phi=(\phi_1,\phi_2,\phi_3)^T\).
2. Pick the normalized eigenvector \(|m_s\rangle\) for the measurement outcome of interest.
3. Form the bra \(\langle m_s|\) by complex conjugation.
4. Compute the amplitude \(\langle m_s|\phi\rangle\).
5. Take the absolute square to get \(P(m_s)=|\langle m_s|\phi\rangle|^2\).
6. For \(m_s=0\), use \(|0\rangle=(0,0,1)^T\), so the amplitude is \(\phi_3\).
7. Keep \(\phi_1,\phi_2,\phi_3\) complex throughout.

Dirac algebra from the relativistic dispersion relation

1. Start from \(i\partial_t\Psi=-i\alpha_i\partial_i\Psi+m\beta\Psi\).
2. Replace spatial derivatives by momentum components for a plane-wave check.
3. Square the operator on the right-hand side.
4. Require the result to reproduce \(\omega^2=k^2+m^2\).
5. Force the square terms to give \(k_i^2\) and \(m^2\), hence \(\alpha_i^2=\beta^2=1\).
6. Force the mixed terms to vanish, hence \(\{\alpha_i,\alpha_j\}=0\) for \(i\neq j\) and \(\{\alpha_i,\beta\}=0\).
7. Note that three Pauli matrices are enough without \(\beta\), but not enough once a fourth anticommuting matrix is needed.
8. Conclude that the minimal faithful representation is \(4\times4\).

Rest-frame Dirac reduction

1. Set momentum \(k=0\), so all spatial-derivative terms vanish.
2. Reduce the Dirac equation to \(i\partial_t\Psi=m\beta\Psi\).
3. Split the four-component spinor as \(\Psi=(\psi_+,\psi_-)^T\).
4. Use the off-diagonal \(\beta\) to swap the upper and lower blocks.
5. Read off \(i\dot\psi_+=m\psi_-\) and \(i\dot\psi_-=m\psi_+\).
6. Add the two equations to get an equation for \(\psi_++\psi_-\).
7. Subtract the two equations to get an equation for \(\psi_+-\psi_-\).
8. Read the corresponding frequencies \(\omega=\pm m\).
9. Interpret this doubling as positive- and negative-energy branches.
10. Keep the remaining two-component structure inside each branch as the spin doubling.

## Notation Choices

- Use \(x,y\) for generic two-particle labels only in the opening exchange-symmetry discussion.
- Use \(x_1,x_2\) for electron coordinates and \(y_1,y_2\) for proton coordinates in the two-hydrogen section.
- Use \(\psi(x_1,y_1)\) and \(\phi(x_2,y_2)\) for the two one-atom wavefunctions in the general two-atom construction; set \(\phi=\psi\) only when discussing two atoms in the same state.
- Use \(S_i\) for spin operators and \(\sigma_i\) for Pauli matrices.
- State explicitly that \(S_i=\tfrac12\sigma_i\) for spin \(1/2\).
- Set \(\hbar=1\) after the lecture’s opening reminder; keep the initial \(S_z=m\hbar\) only as documentary setup.
- Do not reuse the same symbol \(m\) for both mass and spin projection in the written chapter. Use \(m_s\) for spin projection and \(m\) for particle mass.
- Use \(|\pm z\rangle\), \(|\pm x\rangle\), \(|\pm y\rangle\) for spin-\(\tfrac12\) eigenstates when helpful; otherwise use column vectors directly.
- Use \(\psi_\uparrow(x),\psi_\downarrow(x)\) for the position-dependent spin-\(\tfrac12\) components.
- Use \(\phi_1(x),\phi_2(x),\phi_3(x)\) for the position-dependent spin-1 components, following the lecture’s bosonic \(\phi\)-notation.
- Use \(\Psi\) for the four-component Dirac spinor and \(\psi_\pm\) for its two-component block entries.
- Use spatial index \(i=1,2,3\) with summation convention in the Dirac equation; reserve \(I\) for the \(2\times2\) identity matrix.
- When discussing the rest frame, say explicitly that “zero momentum” means no spatial dependence in the wavefunction.

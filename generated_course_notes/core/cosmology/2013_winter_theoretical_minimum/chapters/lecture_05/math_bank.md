## Definitions And Objects
- \(a(t)\): scale factor.
- \(\rho\): energy density.
- \(P\): pressure.
- \(w\): equation-of-state parameter in \(P=w\rho\).
- \(V\): box volume; later \(V\propto a^3\).
- \(E\): total energy in the box.
- \(A\): area of the moving face of the box.
- \(dx\): small displacement of the face; \(dV=A\,dx\).
- \(\rho_0\): vacuum energy density; “naught” denotes vacuum.
- \(\Lambda\): cosmological constant in Susskind’s normalization \(\Lambda=\dfrac{8\pi G}{3}\rho_0\).
- \(k\in\{+1,0,-1\}\): spatial curvature sign, with \(+1\) spherical, \(0\) flat, \(-1\) hyperbolic.
- \(H=\dot a/a\): Hubble parameter; in the flat pure-vacuum case it is constant.
- \(\epsilon\): energy per photon.
- \(\vec{\pi}\): photon momentum vector in the lecture’s notation.
- \(\pi=|\vec{\pi}|\): magnitude of photon momentum.
- \(\nu\): photon number density.
- \(\theta\): angle between photon direction and the inward normal to the wall.
- \(\mathbf n=(n_x,n_y,n_z)\): unit direction vector for photon motion.
- \(\langle\cdot\rangle\): isotropic angular average.

## Derivation Steps
General density-scaling law from \(P=w\rho\)

1. Start with the thermodynamic work relation \(dE=-P\,dV\).
2. Use \(E=\rho V\).
3. Differentiate: \(dE=\rho\,dV+V\,d\rho\).
4. Substitute into the work relation: \(\rho\,dV+V\,d\rho=-P\,dV\).
5. Insert the equation of state \(P=w\rho\).
6. Rearrange to isolate \(d\rho\): \(V\,d\rho=-(1+w)\rho\,dV\).
7. Divide by \(\rho V\): \(\dfrac{d\rho}{\rho}=-(1+w)\dfrac{dV}{V}\).
8. Recognize logarithmic differentials: \(d(\log\rho)=-(1+w)\,d(\log V)\).
9. Integrate: \(\log\rho=-(1+w)\log V+\text{const.}\).
10. Exponentiate: \(\rho\propto V^{-(1+w)}\).
11. Use isotropic expansion \(V\propto a^3\).
12. Conclude \(\rho\propto a^{-3(1+w)}\).

Matter and radiation as checks

1. Set \(w=0\) for nonrelativistic matter.
2. Obtain \(\rho\propto a^{-3}\).
3. Set \(w=\dfrac13\) for radiation.
4. Obtain \(\rho\propto a^{-3(1+1/3)}=a^{-4}\).

Radiation pressure from photon momentum transfer

1. Introduce \(\epsilon\) as the energy per photon and \(\nu\) as the number density.
2. Use the massless relation \(\epsilon=c\pi\), and with \(c=1\), \(\epsilon=\pi\).
3. Therefore \(\rho=\epsilon\nu\).
4. Consider photons that can hit a wall during a short time \(\Delta t\).
5. For a photon at angle \(\theta\), the relevant slab thickness is \(\Delta x=\Delta t\cos\theta\).
6. A reflected photon changes its normal momentum by \(\Delta\pi_x=2\epsilon\cos\theta\).
7. Divide by \(\Delta t\) to get force per hit.
8. Multiply by the number of photons in the slab \((\Delta x)A\nu\).
9. Correct the count by a factor of \(1/2\), since only photons moving toward the wall hit it.
10. Divide total force by area \(A\) to get pressure.
11. Obtain \(P(\theta)=\rho\cos^2\theta\) for photons with a given angle.
12. Average over directions using isotropy.
13. Use \(n_x=\cos\theta\) and \(n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2=1\).
14. Average and use symmetry: \(\langle n_x^2\rangle=\langle n_y^2\rangle=\langle n_z^2\rangle\).
15. Conclude \(\langle\cos^2\theta\rangle=\dfrac13\).
16. Therefore \(P=\dfrac13\rho\).

Vacuum energy equation of state

1. Take vacuum energy density to be constant: \(\rho=\rho_0\).
2. Then the total energy in volume \(V\) is \(E=\rho_0V\).
3. Differentiate: \(dE=\rho_0\,dV\).
4. Use the equation-of-state form \(P=w\rho_0\).
5. Insert into the work relation: \(dE=-P\,dV=-w\rho_0\,dV\).
6. Compare both expressions for \(dE\).
7. Conclude \(1=-w\), hence \(w=-1\).
8. Therefore \(P=-\rho\).

Flat pure-vacuum cosmology

1. Start from \(\left(\dfrac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2=\Lambda-\dfrac{k}{a^2}\).
2. Set \(k=0\).
3. Obtain \(\dfrac{\dot a}{a}=\sqrt{\Lambda}\) for \(\Lambda>0\).
4. Rewrite as \(\dot a=\sqrt{\Lambda}\,a\).
5. Solve to get \(a(t)=C\,e^{\sqrt{\Lambda}\,t}\).
6. Read off \(H=\dot a/a=\sqrt{\Lambda}\).

Positive-curvature, positive-\(\Lambda\) case

1. Start from \(\left(\dfrac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2=\Lambda-\dfrac{1}{a^2}\).
2. Multiply by \(a^2\): \(\dot a^2=\Lambda a^2-1\).
3. Rearrange: \(\dot a^2-\Lambda a^2=-1\).
4. Interpret this as an energy-conservation-like equation with an upside-down quadratic potential.
5. Infer a bounce-type solution qualitatively.
6. State the exact solution: \(a(t)=\Lambda^{-1/2}\cosh(\sqrt{\Lambda}\,t)\).

Negative-\(\Lambda\) cases

1. If \(\Lambda<0\) and \(k=+1\), the right-hand side of the Friedmann equation is negative while the left-hand side is nonnegative.
2. Therefore there is no solution for the positively curved case.
3. Set \(k=-1\) and rescale to \(|\Lambda|=1\).
4. Obtain \(\left(\dfrac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2=-1+\dfrac{1}{a^2}\).
5. Multiply by \(a^2\): \(\dot a^2=1-a^2\).
6. Rearrange: \(\dot a^2+a^2=1\).
7. Interpret this as a harmonic-oscillator energy equation.
8. Conclude that the universe expands from \(a=0\), turns around, and re-collapses.
